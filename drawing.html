My drawing skills:-

It was an ordinary day in school when our teacher gave us a homework assignment that sounded very simple: make a drawing at home and bring it to class. I remember feeling both excited and nervous because, although I loved to draw, I was never very good at it. Still, I sat at my desk that evening, opened my notebook, and tried my best to create something meaningful. I picked up my pencil and began sketching, but the harder I tried, the worse it seemed to get. The shapes looked strange, the lines were uneven, and the picture made no sense at all. After struggling for some time, I finally gave up, thinking, Well, at least I have something to show tomorrow.

The next morning, when I entered the classroom, I could see that many of my classmates had made colorful, creative drawings. Some had used crayons, some had added neat outlines, and their notebooks looked impressive. I, on the other hand, quietly placed my notebook on the desk, hoping that the teacher would not notice my lack of skill. But of course, teachers always notice.

When it was my turn, I handed my notebook over to her. She opened it, looked at my drawing for just a second, and without saying a single word, she picked up her red marker and drew a big cross over it. Then she said loudly enough for the whole class to hear, “This is not a drawing. You have not done your work properly.” The class immediately burst into laughter. My classmates pointed, giggled, and whispered among themselves. My face burned with embarrassment. I wanted to hide under the desk, to disappear, to never open that notebook again.

For the rest of the day, I could not concentrate on anything. The image of that red cross stayed in my mind, and the sound of the class laughing at me echoed in my ears. It felt as if all the effort I had put in at home had no value. But instead of giving up, something inside me changed that day. I thought to myself, If my teacher and my classmates think I cannot draw, I will prove them wrong.

That evening, after school, I took out a fresh sheet of paper. I sharpened my pencils, grabbed my colors, and started again. This time, I was patient. I carefully observed pictures, I erased mistakes, and I spent hours making the details better. Slowly, line by line, shape by shape, my drawing began to improve. For the first time, I felt proud of what I was creating. It was not perfect, but compared to my old messy sketch, this new one looked alive.

Over the next few days, I kept practicing. Every night after finishing homework, I would sit down and make another sketch. Sometimes it was a flower, sometimes a tree, sometimes a small house with clouds and the sun above it. Each drawing was better than the last. I realized that art was not just about talent — it was about practice, patience, and the courage to keep trying even after failure.

Finally, after a week of practice, I decided to show my new drawing in class. I waited for the right moment. When the teacher asked students to display some of their work, I quietly raised my hand and walked to her desk with my notebook. The same teacher who had once crossed out my drawing with a red marker looked at me with surprise. She slowly opened my notebook and looked at the new drawing. For a few seconds, she did not say a word. The class, which was expecting another laugh, went completely silent. Then, instead of making fun, my classmates leaned forward to see what I had drawn.

The teacher’s face softened, and she simply nodded. There was no red cross this time. There was no insult, no laughter. In fact, the silence itself felt like my victory. The teacher’s words had stopped, and my drawing had spoken louder than anything she could have said.

That day I learned an important lesson: failure is not the end. Sometimes failure is the beginning of improvement. Being laughed at once gave me the strength to practice until I was able to silence the laughter. The red cross that once embarrassed me became the motivation that pushed me to draw better. And even though I may never become a professional artist, that experience taught me to never give up and to always believe in the power of trying again.
