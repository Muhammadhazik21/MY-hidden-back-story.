My dream of successfull programer:-

When I was a kid, everyone used to ask me the same question: “What do you want to become when you grow up?” And like many others, my answer was simple — “I want to become a doctor.”

Back then, the idea of being a doctor felt noble and powerful. Saving lives, wearing a white coat, and being respected by everyone — it sounded like the perfect dream. For years, I held on to that ambition, imagining myself with a stethoscope around my neck, helping patients in a big hospital.

But everything began to change when I reached class 8–9.

At that time, I was introduced to the world of computers in a way I had never seen before. Until then, computers were just for games, movies, or typing school projects. But suddenly, I discovered that computers could do something far more magical — they could follow commands, they could think, they could solve problems.

The first time I saw a simple program run — maybe just printing “Hello World” on the screen — I felt something click inside me. It wasn’t just text on a screen. It was like a door opening into another universe.

Slowly, my interest shifted. While others in my class still dreamed of becoming doctors, engineers, or teachers, I found myself drawn deeper into the world of programming.

At first, it was confusing. I remember staring at the code, not knowing why there were so many symbols, semicolons, brackets, and capital letters. Sometimes even a single missing semicolon could break the whole program. I used to feel frustrated, but every time I solved the error, the happiness was unmatched.

This struggle gave me something that my “doctor dream” never did — a sense of adventure. Coding was like solving puzzles, unlocking mysteries step by step. Each bug I fixed felt like a small victory, and each program I completed felt like creating something from nothing.

I started imagining myself not in a hospital, but in front of a computer screen, writing lines of code that could change lives in a different way. I realized that while doctors save lives with medicine, programmers change lives with technology. And maybe, in this modern world, technology was just another form of healing.

Day by day, my passion grew. I started exploring languages like Java, Javascript, Python. Each one was like learning a new language of the future, and every “if-else” statement, every “loop,” every “class and object” gave me a strange kind of joy.

When my classmates talked about biology, I listened. But my heart wasn’t in it anymore. While they memorized the human body, I was busy memorizing syntax. While they dreamed of surgeries, I dreamed of building apps, websites, and maybe even AI systems that could make the future brighter.

By the time I finished class 9, my dream had completely changed. The white coat of a doctor no longer tempted me. Instead, the glow of a computer screen, the power of writing my own code, and the thrill of becoming a programmer had taken over my heart.

And I knew, deep inside, that this wasn’t just a phase. This was my new identity.

I wasn’t going to save lives with injections or medicines anymore. I was going to build a future with logic and code.
